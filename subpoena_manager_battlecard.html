Top 5 Winning Differentiators

🤖 AI That Actually Does the Work — Not Just Routes a Form⌄
Subpoena Manager's AI captures requests from fragmented channels, extracts deadlines and critical details automatically, creates a structured record, routes work, and triggers follow-up — without a human initiating each step. ServiceNow and other workflow tools require someone to fill out a form to start the process. Subpoena Manager starts the moment the subpoena arrives.
⚖️ Governed Control — The Boundary That Builds Trust⌄
Attorneys and legal ops leaders remain in control of every legal decision — approvals, scope, privilege, collection authorization, production. This is not a limitation; it is what makes this AI legal teams can deploy without legal risk. Generic automation tools either overclaim autonomy or force attorneys to manage every step manually. Subpoena Manager finds the right boundary by design.
📋 Legal-Specific AI Extraction — Not a Generic Form Parser⌄
The AI is tuned to extract the details that matter in legal requests: deadlines, requesting parties, request types, jurisdiction, key metadata. Generic OCR tools or workflow intake forms require humans to identify and enter this information manually. Subpoena Manager surfaces it automatically from the subpoena document itself — reducing the first 30–60 minutes of manual work at intake.
🔗 Platform Front End — Not an Island⌄
Subpoena Manager creates a structured front door to Exterro's downstream legal hold, collection, review, and production workflows. No point solution or generic workflow tool can do this. For existing Exterro customers, that means immediate value on both sides of the handoff. For new buyers, it means a clear path to full legal operations coverage on a single platform.
📊 Defensible Audit Trail from First Receipt⌄
Every action is logged from the moment a subpoena is captured — who touched it, when, what was approved, what was escalated. Spreadsheets have no audit trail. Email threads are scattered and incomplete. Generic workflow tools may log actions but were not designed for the defensibility requirements of legal response. Subpoena Manager preserves a complete, auditable record that holds up in regulatory or litigation contexts.
⏱️ 90 Minutes to 5 Minutes — Proof of Value Is Immediate⌄
The ROI case is concrete and fast. A subpoena process that once required roughly 90 minutes of reading, entering, triaging, and coordinating can be compressed to as little as 5 minutes. At ~20 subpoenas per month, that is nearly a full work week recovered monthly. No configuration project or internal build can get there this quickly — and no generic workflow tool can match the specificity of legal-tuned AI extraction.

Manual Process / Spreadsheets & Email

The most common competitive situation — not a vendor, but a process. This is where the ROI case wins. Lead with the time math, then move to defensibility and scale.

📋

Manual / Spreadsheets / Email

Shared inboxes · Excel/Google Sheets · Calendar reminders · PDF tools · Individual follow-up
HIGH THREAT

Subpoena Manager Wins When...

  • ROI math lands — 90 min → 5 min; ~1 work week per month recovered at 20 subpoenas
  • Volume is increasing and the current process can't scale without adding headcount
  • Leadership is asking for process visibility or reporting that spreadsheets can't provide
  • A missed deadline, compliance event, or process failure has created urgency
  • GC or Deputy GC wants a defensible, auditable process — not tribal knowledge

The Manual Process Problem

  • No audit trail — what was approved, when, by whom is unclear or undocumented
  • Scales with headcount — volume increases mean hiring, not efficiency
  • Knowledge lives with individuals — turnover creates institutional risk
  • Visibility is manual — status requires asking or building a report by hand
  • Follow-up depends on memory and personal effort — a single distraction breaks the chain
  • "Zero cost" is a myth — calculate attorney time, paralegal hours, and deadline risk

ROI Conversation Starters

  • How much time does each subpoena take from receipt to confirmed ownership? (Build to the 90-min baseline)
  • At your current volume, how many attorney or paralegal hours per month go to subpoena intake mechanics?
  • If you had to demonstrate your process for a specific subpoena to a regulator next week — how long would that take?
  • When the person who manages subpoena tracking is out — what happens to the process?
Killer Question

"If someone on your team left tomorrow, would the subpoena process continue to run the same way? Or would it depend on whoever was left knowing the unwritten rules?"

The ROI Math — Build It in the Room

Use this framework: (Subpoenas per month) × (minutes per subpoena) ÷ 60 = hours lost monthly. At 20 subpoenas × 90 min = 30 hours = nearly a full work week. At a fully-loaded paralegal rate, that is a concrete dollar figure the business understands.
⏱️

Time per Subpoena

Ask the prospect to estimate their own number. Most start at "maybe 30 minutes" and arrive at 60–90 minutes when you walk through each step: reading, entering, routing, follow-up, status tracking.

📅

Monthly Volume

Ask for monthly subpoena count. Remind them to include all request types that go through a similar process — not just formal court subpoenas. Volume is usually higher than the initial estimate.

💰

Fully-Loaded Cost

Hours × (paralegal or attorney loaded rate) = monthly cost of the current process. Add risk premium for missed deadlines, defensibility gaps, and scale failure. The ROI math closes fast.

TCO Conversation Starters

  • If a regulator asked you to demonstrate the audit trail from your internal tool, what would they see?
Killer Question

"When the person who built your intake tool leaves — and they will eventually — what happens to your subpoena process? Is that a risk your GC is aware of?"

Build vs. Buy Framework: Cost to build (IT hours × rate) + Cost to maintain (ongoing IT support) + Risk cost (missed deadline, defensibility gap, rebuild if builder leaves) vs. Subpoena Manager subscription + time-to-value. The math almost always favors buy when the full cost of build is visible.

Master Talk Track — Any Competitive Situation

1

Open with the Problem — Not the Product

Start with what you heard in discovery. "You mentioned that intake is still manual — someone reads the subpoena, enters the details, and figures out who it goes to. Walk me through what that actually looks like on a busy day." Let them describe the pain before you describe the solution.

Make the Manual Process Visible

Walk through the six steps of a typical manual process: receive → read → enter → route → follow-up → track. Ask them to estimate time at each step. "At your volume, that math adds up fast. At 20 subpoenas a month, you're spending roughly a full work week just on intake mechanics."

3

Introduce the Shift — Not the Features

"What Subpoena Manager does is take the first four or five steps of that process and run them automatically — capture, extract, structure, route, and trigger follow-up — before anyone has to touch it. 4

Make the Boundary Explicit — Before They Ask

"The part I want to be clear about: the AI handles the operational work, not the legal work. Approvals, scope decisions, privilege calls, collection authorization — that stays with your attorneys and legal ops leaders. That's intentional. It's what makes this process defensible."

5

Show the Demo — Follow the Workflow

Demo sequence: (1) Subpoena arrives — show auto-capture. (2) Details extracted — show structured record with deadline, parties, type. (3) Ownership assigned — show routing confirmation. (4) Follow-up triggered — show cross-dept notification. (5) Human review gate — show approval before action. End on the dashboard: "This is what your GC sees. Close with the Platform Story

"This is the front door to your legal operations workflow. When a subpoena triggers a legal hold, that handoff happens from a structured record — not an email. Landmines & Traps

Situations where Subpoena Manager loses, mistakes that kill deals, and competitor traps to anticipate. Know these before any competitive evaluation.

Where Subpoena Manager Can Lose — Know Your Gaps

⚠️

Very Low Volume (<5 subpoenas/month)

The ROI case weakens significantly at very low volumes. The time math doesn't produce the "nearly a full work week" proof point. Qualify carefully — if the real pain is defensibility or process inconsistency, those arguments still hold. If it's purely time savings, manage expectations on ROI math.

🔒

AI Adoption Blocked at the Enterprise Level

Some organizations have blanket AI moratoriums in place, particularly in regulated industries. Understand the policy before you lead with AI language. If AI adoption is formally blocked, the sale stalls regardless of the product's merit. Surface this in discovery.

🏛️

IT-Driven Evaluation Locked to ServiceNow

If IT has already decided the answer is ServiceNow and legal ops does not have independent buying authority, you may lose on mandate before the evaluation starts. Multi-thread to Legal Ops and GC who feel the pain that IT's tool doesn't solve. Get Legal in the room before the decision is made for them.

💸

Price-Only Evaluation

If the evaluation is purely on license cost and the prospect refuses to consider TCO, time-to-value, or risk — you may not win against a "free" manual process or a spreadsheet. Reframe to true cost: attorney time, paralegal hours, missed deadline risk, and the cost of one defensibility failure. If they won't engage with total cost, qualify appetite.

Common Mistakes That Kill Subpoena Manager Deals

🚫

Leading with AgentMode as the Product

AgentMode is the strategic bridge — not what is available today. If you lead with AgentMode capability and the prospect asks to see it, you lose credibility. Always lead with Subpoena Manager as the product being sold now. Use AgentMode only as the vision layer after the product story is established.

🚫

Overselling AI Autonomy

The boundary between what the AI does and what attorneys control is a feature, not a limitation. If you imply the AI is more autonomous than it is — making decisions, replacing attorney review — you create legal team resistance that is very hard to walk back. Be explicit about the boundary proactively.

🚫

Single-Threading to Legal Ops

Legal Ops may champion, but GC controls budget and risk appetite. eDiscovery managers feel downstream pain. Paralegals validate that it changes their daily work. Multi-thread early — the sale closes faster when the GC sees defensibility value and Legal Ops sees ROI simultaneously.
