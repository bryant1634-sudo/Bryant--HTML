Sales Play · Overview
Sales Play

Exterro Subpoena Manager

The first live expression of governed autonomous AI for legal operations. This play is for AEs and SEs selling into corporate legal departments that need a faster, more consistent, and more defensible subpoena response process.

Core Positioning: Subpoena Manager begins delivering governed autonomous AI for legal operations — AI that captures requests, extracts critical details, creates structured records, routes work, triggers follow-up, and preserves human oversight where legal judgment matters.

The Problem We Solve

📥

Fragmented Intake

Subpoenas arrive through email, mail, portals, and service of process — each requiring manual identification, reading, and re-entry into disconnected systems.

⏱️

Clerical Drag

Teams spend 1–1.5 hours per subpoena on intake, triage, and coordination. At ~20 subpoenas per month, that is nearly a full work week every month lost to manual process.

🔀

Inconsistent Routing

Ownership is often unclear. Follow-up with HR, Claims, and other departments depends on individual effort, not a controlled workflow.

📊

Poor Visibility

Status lives in email threads, spreadsheets, and personal notes. Leadership has no reliable view of workload, bottlenecks, or turnaround time.

⚖️

Defensibility Risk

Disconnected tracking makes it harder to prove what happened, when, and who approved it — a liability in any regulated or high-stakes legal environment.

🧠

Attorney Time Wasted

Attorneys spend time managing process mechanics — reading requests, chasing follow-up — instead of focusing on legal judgment, scope, and response strategy.

That distinction is a feature, not a limitation — it is what makes this AI legal teams can use with confidence.

Sales Play · Discovery
Discovery Questions

Open the Conversation

Questions that surface real pain, establish a baseline, and create urgency without feeling like an interrogation. Use 3–4 per call — not all at once.

Process & Operations

Q1 — INTAKE

How are subpoenas received and logged today? Walk me through what happens from the moment one arrives.

Listening for: email, manual entry, spreadsheet. The more manual the description, the stronger the fit.
Q2 — TIME

How much time does your team typically spend on intake, triage, and initial routing for each subpoena?

Listening for: anything over 30 minutes is a strong signal. The 90-minute benchmark is your proof point anchor.
Q3 — ROUTING

How do you determine who handles each request — and what happens when that person is out or unavailable?

Listening for: "it depends," "we figure it out," or a named individual. Single points of failure are a strong buying signal.
Q4 — FOLLOW-UP

When you need a response from HR, Claims, or another department, how do you track that follow-up today?

Listening for: manual reminders, email chasing, or "it falls through the cracks." Cross-department friction is a common accelerant.

Visibility & Defensibility

Q5 — VISIBILITY

If your GC asked you right now how many open subpoenas you have and where each one stands, how quickly could you answer that?

This question often lands hard with Legal Ops leaders. If the honest answer involves pulling from multiple places, you have a clear gap to solve.
Q6 — REPORTING

How do you currently report on subpoena volumes, turnaround time, and bottlenecks to leadership?

Listening for: manual reports, spreadsheet compilation, or "we don't really have good reporting right now."
Q7 — DEFENSIBILITY

If you had to demonstrate the process you followed on a specific subpoena — who touched it, when, and who approved each step — how easy is that today?

This surfaces the audit trail gap. Regulated industries respond strongly to this. Legal teams in litigation also feel it acutely.
Q8 — DOWNSTREAM

When a subpoena triggers a legal hold or collection, what does that handoff look like today?

Opens the platform conversation. If they're already on Exterro downstream, this shows immediate integration value.

Qualification & Timing

Q9 — VOLUME

Roughly how many subpoenas does your team handle in a typical month? Does that volume fluctuate seasonally?

Best fit: 10+ per month. ROI math gets compelling fast at 20+. High variation also signals a workflow standardization need.
Q10 — URGENCY

Has anything happened recently — a missed deadline, a compliance review, or a leadership push for efficiency — that's made this a priority to solve?

Buying urgency accelerates when there is a recent pain event. If not, you may need to create urgency through the ROI conversation.

Objection Handling

Handle the Hard Questions

The objections you will actually hear — with direct responses that keep the conversation moving. Click to expand.

AI & Trust Objections

"I don't want AI making legal decisions." ▾
That's exactly the right instinct — and it is also how Subpoena Manager is designed. The AI handles the operational mechanics: capturing requests, extracting details, creating records, routing work, and triggering follow-up. Attorneys and legal operations leaders remain in control of approvals, scope decisions, privilege calls, collection authorization, and production decisions. That boundary is a feature, not a limitation. It is what makes this something legal teams can deploy with confidence.
"We're not ready for AI in our legal workflow." ▾
That's a fair position for speculative or black-box AI. But Subpoena Manager is practical execution inside a workflow your team already runs. It is not asking you to change how you make legal decisions — it is taking the repetitive administrative work off your team's plate. The question is not whether you're ready for AI in the abstract. It's whether you're ready to stop paying your team to retype information into three systems.
"How accurate is the AI? What happens when it gets something wrong?" ▾
Human review is built into every stage. Before anything moves into preservation, collection, or production, a person reviews and approves. The AI is doing the extraction and routing — not the legal judgment. If it makes an error at the extraction stage, a human catches it before it matters. The risk of an AI extraction error is far lower than the risk of a manual process that misses a deadline or routes incorrectly because someone was out of the office.

Value & ROI Objections

"We already have a process that works." ▾
How long does it take your team from the moment a subpoena arrives to confirmed ownership and active routing? If the honest answer is 30 minutes or more per request — and most teams tell us it is closer to 90 minutes — the process may be functional, but it's not efficient. The question is whether that time could be better spent on the work that actually requires legal expertise. A process that "works" and a process that is optimal are two different things.
"We don't have enough volume to justify this." ▾
The ROI math at 20 subpoenas per month compresses to nearly a full work week recovered. But volume is not the only driver. If intake is inconsistent, routing is ad hoc, or defensibility is a concern, the value is there regardless. One missed deadline or a compliance challenge on a poorly documented process can cost far more than the investment. What does your current process cost when something goes wrong?
"Is this just a Request Manager with an AI label on it?" ▾
Subpoena Manager is the first live expression of a governed autonomous execution model — not a rebrand of existing workflow features. The difference is what the AI does: it captures incoming requests from multiple channels, extracts the critical details automatically, creates structured records, routes work based on the extracted content, and triggers follow-up without manual intervention. That is not a form submission workflow. It is governed autonomous execution in a legal operations context.

Competitive & Timing Objections

"We're already looking at [generic workflow tool / ServiceNow / competitor]." ▾
Generic workflow tools are built for general business processes — they require significant configuration to approximate what Subpoena Manager does out of the box for legal. More importantly, they don't have AI-driven extraction tuned for legal requests, they don't connect to downstream legal hold, collection, and production workflows, and they don't provide the defensibility and auditability that legal teams require. The question to ask: is that tool built for legal operations, or are you adapting a general tool to fit a legal problem?
"We want to wait and see how AgentMode develops before we commit." ▾
AgentMode is the strategic bridge to what comes next. Subpoena Manager is live today and delivers value in a workflow you already run. Waiting for AgentMode means continuing to spend 60–90 minutes per subpoena on clerical work while the competitive gap widens. The teams that start now will have structured data, trained workflows, and operational baseline in place when the broader platform capabilities arrive. Waiting is a cost.
"This is interesting but it's not a priority right now." ▾
That's a fair place to be. Let me ask one thing: what would make this a priority? In most cases it is a missed deadline, a compliance review, or leadership asking legal ops to demonstrate efficiency gains. If none of those have happened yet, they eventually will. The cost of waiting is that the manual process keeps running — and the risk of something going wrong compounds every month. Is there a timeline we should revisit?
