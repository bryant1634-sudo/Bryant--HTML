Market Context — Q1 through Q6

📄Three Subpoena Types
Subpoena duces tecum: Produce documents/records — no testimony
Subpoena ad testificandum: Appear and provide testimony
Deposition subpoena: Appear at deposition, may also produce documents
⚡Critical Reps Must Know
Subpoenas are unpredictable — disrupt legal team priorities
Even when objecting, data must still be preserved
Breakdowns occur at intake, routing, follow-up — not just review
Legal teams need workflow control, not just document tools

Sales Opportunity — Q7 through Q10

🔁The Core Reframe
Buyers focus on: collection, review, redaction, production
Exterro expands to: intake, extraction, routing, follow-up, audit trail
The reframe line: "Modern eDiscovery manages documents. Exterro manages the subpoena response workflow."
💡The Core Insight
Memorize this: "A subpoena response problem does not start at document review. It starts the instant the subpoena is received."
This is the single most important line in the learning guide

Positioning — Q11 through Q14

🎯4 Positioning Statements
Core: Full context conversations
Short: Email, cold outreach — "starts much earlier"
Executive (GC/CLO): Cost, risk, and control issue
Practitioner (Paralegal): Replace spreadsheet tracking
🥊Competitive Reframe — 1st Question
Ask: "Does the solution start at subpoena intake or only after documents are ready for discovery?"
This immediately reveals whether the buyer's tool covers the front end
Never compete only on review features — reframe to full lifecycle

Buyer Pain Points — Q15 through Q18

🔴6 Pain Points at a Glance
Pain 1: Subpoenas arrive unpredictably → fire drills
Pain 2: Intake is manual and inconsistent → re-keying
Pain 3: Routing depends on email and memory → chasing
Pain 4: Sensitive data and privilege must be protected
Pain 5: Outside counsel dependency → avoidable cost
Pain 6: No clean audit trail → defensibility risk
👂What to Listen For
"Spreadsheets" → Pain 2 or Pain 6
"Email follow-up" / "chasing HR" → Pain 3
"Easier to send to outside counsel" → Pain 5
"I'd have to dig through emails" → Pain 6
"Came out of nowhere" → Pain 1

Discovery Questions — Q22 through Q24

⭐Best Closing Question — Memorize
"If you could redesign subpoena response, would you start by improving document review, or would you first fix the intake, routing, deadline, and follow-up process?"
Forces buyer to choose — almost always confirms front-end is the priority
📊Talk Track Usage
30 sec: Cold calls, conferences, hallway conversations
60 sec: Discovery calls, first meetings, follow-up emails
Executive: GC, CLO, C-suite — cost, risk, and control framing
After 60-sec track, ask: "Does any of that resonate with how your team handles subpoena response today?"

Objection Handling — Q25 through Q27

1"We already have an eDiscovery platform."
Your Response

"That's important for collection, review, and production. The question is whether your process is controlled before discovery begins."

Follow-Up Question

"Does your platform give you a complete workflow record from receipt through production, or does early-stage work still happen in email and spreadsheets?"

2"We use outside counsel for subpoenas."
Your Response

"That makes sense for complex matters. But many teams ask whether routine work can be handled internally with better process support."

Follow-Up Question

"Which types would you consider bringing in-house if the intake, routing, and tracking workflow were easier to manage?"

3"Subpoenas aren't a big enough volume problem."
Your Response

"Volume matters, but so does risk and disruption. Even occasional subpoenas create fire drills without a consistent process."

Follow-Up Question

"When subpoenas do arrive, do they follow a consistent workflow, or does the team have to recreate the process each time?"

4"We already track subpoenas in spreadsheets."
Your Response

"Spreadsheets track basic info but don't manage workflow, automate follow-up, enforce approvals, or create a complete defensible record."

Follow-Up Question

"If a response were challenged, would the spreadsheet show who did what, when, what was reviewed, approved, and produced?"

5"We're cautious about AI in legal workflows."
Your Response

"That caution is appropriate. Exterro uses governed AI — the AI handles repetitive mechanics; attorneys control every legal decision."

Follow-Up Question

"Where would you be comfortable using AI to reduce repetitive work, and where would you require human review before action is taken?"

Sales Plays & Do/Don't — Q28 through Q30

🎮4 Sales Plays — Triggers
Play 1: "We use outside counsel" → Bring In-House
Play 2: "We use spreadsheets" → Replace Spreadsheet Tracking
Play 3: Has eDiscovery but manual intake → Connect Intake to Discovery
Play 4: "Subpoenas are disruptive / ad hoc" → Reduce Fire Drills
⚠️Critical Never-Do Rules
Never say: AI makes legal decisions
Never claim: Exterro eliminates outside counsel entirely
Never position: subpoena response as just administrative intake
Never reduce the conversation to document review alone
Never use unsupported performance claims
✓ Do This
  • Use subpoena response as a cost, risk, and workflow modernization conversation
  • Emphasize the problem starts before eDiscovery review
  • Ask about intake, routing, deadlines, and auditability
  • Keep human oversight central to every message
  • Use cost, speed, consistency, and defensibility together
✗ Don't Do This
  • Don't claim Exterro eliminates the need for outside counsel
  • Don't suggest AI makes legal decisions
  • Don't reduce the conversation to document review alone
  • Don't use unsupported performance claims
  • Don't position subpoena response as just administrative intake
