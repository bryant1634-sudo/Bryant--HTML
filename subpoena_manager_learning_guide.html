Module 1 · Foundation

Market Context:
Why This Conversation Matters

Third-party subpoenas are often unpredictable, disruptive, and costly for in-house legal teams. Understanding the market context helps you lead the conversation with authority — and position Exterro around a genuine operational need, not a product feature.

What Are Third-Party Subpoenas?

Setting the stage for the buyer conversation

📄 Document Subpoena

Requires the recipient to produce documents, records, or physical evidence. No testimony required.

🎤 Witness Subpoena

Compels a witness to appear in court or at a deposition to provide testimony.

📋 Deposition Subpoena

Calls for a witness to appear at a deposition to testify and potentially produce documents.

Key Context

Companies often receive subpoenas from parties in litigation they are not involved in. Requests come from regulatory organizations — SEC, DOJ, industry regulators, government agencies, and grand juries. The information sought may be confidential, privileged, or sensitive.

Common Subpoena Scenarios

Know what triggers these conversations

🏛️ Regulatory Investigations

SEC, Department of Justice, or industry-specific regulatory bodies seeking information as part of an investigation into conduct or compliance matters.

⚖️ Third-Party Litigation

Other parties in active litigation seeking customer records, contracts, or communications that the organization may hold.

👥 Employee Matters

Employees involved in divorce, debt, or personal lawsuits where their employer holds relevant records.

💡 IP Disputes & M&A

Patent, trademark, and copyright disputes, plus mergers and acquisitions seeking due diligence materials or financial records.

What Reps Must Know Cold

These points should be second nature before any buyer conversation

  • Subpoenas are unpredictable. They arrive unexpectedly and disrupt planned legal team priorities immediately.
  • Legal teams must act fast. Understand the request, assess burden, determine data access, and protect privilege — quickly.
  • Subpoena response resembles litigation response. Teams may need to preserve data, issue legal holds, collect, review, and produce.
  • Ignoring a subpoena creates serious risk. Noncompliance leads to legal consequences, reputational damage, and escalation.
  • Even objections don't stop preservation. Teams may seek to quash or narrow scope, but potentially responsive data must still be protected.
  • Routine work can be handled internally. Over-reliance on outside counsel creates delays and unnecessary expense. Process control is the enabler.
  • The breakdown starts earlier than eDiscovery. Intake, routing, follow-up, and approval failures happen before review and production begin.

The Big Sales Opportunity

Legal teams want to bring more subpoena response work in-house. But many still struggle because the early stages are fragmented, manual, and hard to audit. That's where Exterro wins.

The Core Insight

"A subpoena response problem does not start at document review. It starts the instant the subpoena is received."

Where the Pain Actually Lives

The early stages where most teams are still completely manual

🔴 What breaks down at intake

  • Manual intake — no structured capture
  • Re-keying information into spreadsheets or matter systems
  • Missing or inconsistent deadline capture
  • Unclear ownership from the moment of receipt
  • Manual routing to HR, claims, records, IT, finance
  • Chasing internal responses by email
  • No unified status visibility across the team
  • No reliable workflow audit trail

🟣 Where buyers think the problem lives

  • Collection efficiency
  • Review speed
  • PII redaction capability
  • Early case assessment
  • Collaboration on review
  • Production quality
  • Outside counsel collaboration

Your job: expand the conversation to everything before collection begins.

Module 8 · Tools

Objection Handling

Five objections you will hear in every subpoena response conversation. The goal is never to argue — it's to redirect to a question that surfaces the gap the buyer hasn't fully considered.

Objection "We already have an eDiscovery platform." ▾
Your Response

That's important, especially for collection, review, redaction, and production. The question is whether your process is controlled before discovery begins. How do subpoenas get captured, classified, routed, tracked, followed up on, and approved before documents are collected and produced?

Follow-Up Question

"Does your current platform give you a complete workflow record from receipt through production, or does the early-stage work still happen in email and spreadsheets?"

Objection "We use outside counsel for subpoenas." ▾
Your Response

Outside counsel can be essential for complex or high-risk matters. But many organizations are asking whether routine subpoena work can be handled more efficiently in-house. The opportunity is to reserve outside counsel for the matters that truly require legal strategy while giving the internal team better workflow control for repeatable requests.

Follow-Up Question

"Which subpoena types would you consider bringing in-house if the intake, routing, tracking, and review workflow were easier to manage?"

Objection "Subpoenas aren't a big enough volume problem for us." ▾
Your Response

Volume is one part of the business case, but risk and disruption also matter. Even occasional subpoenas can create fire drills if the process depends on manual intake, email follow-up, and unclear ownership. A single mishandled subpoena — privilege inadvertently waived, deadline missed, audit trail missing — carries significant organizational risk.

Follow-Up Question

"When subpoenas do arrive, do they follow a consistent workflow, or does the team have to recreate the process each time?"

Objection "We already track subpoenas in spreadsheets." ▾
Your Response

Spreadsheets can track basic information, but they don't manage workflow, automate follow-up, enforce approvals, connect to discovery activity, or create a complete defensible record of the response. They also tend to live with one person and become outdated the moment something changes.

Follow-Up Question

"If a response were challenged, would the spreadsheet show who did what, when they did it, what was reviewed, what was approved, and what was produced?"

Objection "We're cautious about AI in legal workflows." ▾
Your Response

That caution is appropriate — and it's the right instinct for legal workflows. Exterro's message is not unchecked automation. It's governed AI-assisted workflow, with human oversight where legal judgment matters. The AI handles the repetitive operational mechanics: capture, extraction, routing, follow-up. Attorneys and legal operations leaders remain in control of every decision that requires legal expertise.

Follow-Up Question

"Where would you be comfortable using AI to reduce repetitive work, and where would you require human review before action is taken?"

Training Exercises

Three exercises to practice before your next subpoena response conversation. Write your answer, then reveal the suggested response. There are no wrong answers — the goal is to practice the thinking, not memorize a script.

Exercise 1 Identify the Gap
The buyer says: "We already use an eDiscovery platform for subpoenas."
Write three follow-up questions that determine whether the buyer has a complete subpoena workflow or only a downstream discovery tool.
Suggested Responses
  • How do subpoenas get captured and classified before documents are collected? Is there a system for that, or does it happen manually?
  • How are deadlines, assignments, and follow-up tasks tracked across HR, claims, and other departments?
  • Can you see the full process — from receipt through production — in one place, or is the early-stage work still in email and spreadsheets?
Exercise 2 Reframe the Pitch
Convert this feature-based pitch into a business-value pitch.
"Our system uses AI to extract subpoena metadata and route requests automatically."
Suggested Response
  • Subpoena Manager helps legal teams reduce manual intake and routing work by turning each subpoena into a structured, trackable workflow from the instant of receipt — so teams can respond faster while maintaining visibility and control.
  • The AI handles the mechanics: reading, extracting, routing, and triggering follow-up. Your team stays in control of the decisions that require legal judgment.
  • The result: fewer hours lost to clerical work, a more consistent process, and a defensible audit trail — without adding headcount.
Exercise 3 Handle the Outside Counsel Objection
The buyer says: "We just send subpoenas to outside counsel."
Write a response that acknowledges their position, then opens a door to the in-house conversation without being dismissive or pushy.
Suggested Response
  • Start by acknowledging: "That makes complete sense for complex or high-risk matters — outside counsel can be essential there."
  • Then pivot: "What we're seeing more of is legal teams asking whether routine subpoena work can be handled internally with better process support — not because they want to cut outside counsel entirely, but because they want to reserve them for the matters that truly need legal strategy."
  • Then ask: "Which subpoena types do you send out because they truly require outside legal strategy, and which ones go out because the internal process is too manual to manage efficiently?"
